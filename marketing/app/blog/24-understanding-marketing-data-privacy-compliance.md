## What Qualifies as Marketing Data?

Before you can protect customer data, you need to understand what you're actually collecting.

In B2B marketing, **personal data** includes any information that can identify an individual, either on its own or when combined with other data points. This extends far beyond obvious identifiers like names and email addresses.

### Common Types of Marketing Data

**Identity Information:**
- Full names, email addresses, phone numbers
- Job titles, company names, LinkedIn profiles
- IP addresses (which can identify companies or individuals)

**Behavioral Data:**
- Pages viewed and time spent on site
- Content downloaded (whitepapers, case studies, tools)
- Email open rates, click-through rates, and engagement patterns
- Ad impressions and click history

**Technical Data:**
- Device type, browser, operating system
- Geographic location (city, state, country)
- Referral sources and campaign UTM parameters

**Enriched Data:**
- Company size, revenue, industry classification
- Technology stack and purchase intent signals
- Demographic information from third-party sources

This data flows through multiple systems throughout your marketing stack. A prospect might fill out a website form, triggering data capture by your CRM. That information then syncs to your marketing automation platform, email service provider, and ad platforms for retargeting.

**Each transfer point represents a compliance obligation.**

## Why Privacy Regulations Keep Getting Stricter

A decade ago, most businesses collected customer data with minimal oversight. Websites tracked users without disclosure. Email lists grew through purchased contacts. Data brokers sold personal information freely.

Those days are over.

High-profile data breaches, surveillance capitalism concerns, and growing consumer awareness have driven regulators worldwide to impose strict rules on data collection, processing, and storage.

### Key Privacy Regulations Affecting B2B Marketers

**GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)**

Applies to any business processing data of EU residents, regardless of where the company is based. Key requirements:

- **Explicit consent** for non-essential data collection
- **Right to access**: Users can request all data you hold about them
- **Right to deletion**: Users can demand you erase their data
- **Data portability**: Users can request data in machine-readable format
- **Breach notification**: Report breaches within 72 hours

**Penalties**: Up to €20 million or 4% of global annual revenue, whichever is higher.

**CCPA/CPRA (California Consumer Privacy Act/California Privacy Rights Act)**

Covers California residents, with similar regulations spreading to Virginia, Colorado, Connecticut, and Utah. Requirements include:

- **Disclosure** of data collection practices
- **Opt-out rights** for data sales and sharing
- **Access and deletion rights** similar to GDPR
- **Do Not Sell My Personal Information** disclosures

**Penalties**: Up to $7,500 per intentional violation, plus civil lawsuit exposure.

**CAN-SPAM Act (US)**

Governs commercial email, requiring:

- Accurate sender information and subject lines
- Clear opt-out mechanisms honored within 10 days
- Physical mailing address in every email

**Penalties**: Up to $51,744 per violation.

Beyond legal compliance, privacy regulations reflect a broader shift in customer expectations. B2B buyers now expect transparency about data usage—and they'll abandon companies that feel invasive or untrustworthy.

## Common Compliance Mistakes That Put Businesses at Risk

Even well-intentioned marketing teams make mistakes that expose them to regulatory action or customer backlash.

### 1. Collecting Data Without Clear Consent

Embedding tracking pixels without disclosure, pre-checking consent boxes, or using vague language like "by using this site, you agree..." doesn't meet GDPR standards.

**Compliant approach**: Use clear, affirmative consent mechanisms. Cookie banners must allow genuine choice—users should be able to reject tracking without losing site functionality.

### 2. Forgetting to Update Privacy Policies

Your privacy policy from 2020 probably doesn't cover the new marketing tools you've added since then. When you integrate new platforms—like [Cursive's audience builder](https://meetcursive.com/audience-builder)—your privacy disclosures must update to reflect the data flows.

**Compliant approach**: Review privacy policies quarterly. Ensure they accurately describe what data you collect, how you use it, which third parties receive it, and how users can opt out.

### 3. Ignoring Opt-Out Requests

Some businesses treat unsubscribes as "soft" signals, continuing to send "relationship" emails or retarget users with ads. Under GDPR and CCPA, opt-outs must be honored immediately across all channels.

**Compliant approach**: When someone opts out, scrub them from all marketing lists, stop ad retargeting, and suppress them from future uploads. **Cursive automatically syncs opt-out preferences across all 200+ integrated platforms**, ensuring compliance without manual tracking.

### 4. Buying Third-Party Contact Lists

Purchased email lists violate GDPR consent requirements and damage deliverability. Even if a list claims to be "opt-in," you can't prove consent was given for *your* specific use.

**Compliant approach**: Build your audience organically through content, website forms, and events. Use [intent data from Cursive](https://meetcursive.com/intent-audiences) to identify in-market prospects, then reach them through compliant channels like ads and direct mail before asking for email opt-in.

### 5. Sharing Data Without Proper Agreements

When you integrate your CRM with third-party tools, you're technically "sharing" customer data. GDPR requires Data Processing Agreements (DPAs) with every vendor that processes personal information on your behalf.

**Compliant approach**: Ensure all vendors in your marketing stack provide signed DPAs. Cursive provides enterprise-grade data processing agreements for all customers, covering our role as a data processor and our security commitments.

### 6. Retaining Data Indefinitely

Storing customer data "just in case" violates data minimization principles. GDPR requires you to keep data only as long as necessary for the stated purpose.

**Compliant approach**: Implement data retention schedules. For example, delete inactive leads after 2 years, archive closed deals after 5 years, and purge non-converting website visitors after 12 months.

## Practical Steps to Ensure Ongoing Compliance

Privacy compliance isn't a one-time project—it's an ongoing operational discipline.

### 1. Conduct a Data Inventory Audit

Document every place customer data lives:

- What data do you collect? (Behavioral, contact info, firmographic)
- Where is it stored? (CRM, marketing automation, analytics, data warehouses)
- Who has access? (Sales, marketing, customer success, third-party vendors)
- How long do you keep it? (Retention schedules)

This audit reveals gaps, overlaps, and unnecessary data collection that increases risk.

### 2. Map Data Flows Between Systems

Create a visual map showing how data moves through your stack:

Website form → CRM → Marketing automation → Email platform → Ad retargeting

Each arrow represents a compliance touchpoint. Does the handoff preserve consent? Is the receiving system configured correctly? Are DPAs in place?

### 3. Implement Privacy by Design

Evaluate every new marketing initiative through a privacy lens:

- Does this campaign require new data collection?
- Do we have proper consent for this use?
- What's the minimum data needed to achieve the goal?
- How will we handle opt-outs?

Making privacy a default consideration prevents problems before they start.

### 4. Train Your Marketing Team

Privacy compliance fails when teams don't understand the rules. Regular training ensures everyone knows:

- What data is considered personal information
- When and how to obtain consent
- How to handle opt-out requests
- Which data practices violate regulations

Quarterly 30-minute sessions keep compliance top-of-mind without overwhelming teams.

### 5. Monitor Third-Party Vendors

Your compliance obligations extend to every vendor processing customer data. Annually review:

- Active integrations (do you still use all of them?)
- DPAs and security certifications (are they current?)
- Privacy policy updates (did vendors change terms?)
- Breach notifications (did any vendors experience incidents?)

**Cursive's compliance team monitors all 200+ integrated platforms** for security incidents, privacy policy changes, and regulatory updates—alerting customers proactively when action is required.

### 6. Establish Incident Response Procedures

When something goes wrong—a data breach, accidental email to the wrong list, or vendor compromise—you need clear protocols:

- Who assesses the severity?
- How quickly must you notify affected users?
- What communication goes to regulators?
- How do you prevent recurrence?

Document these procedures and test them annually with tabletop exercises.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is marketing data privacy compliance?

Marketing data privacy compliance means collecting, storing, and using customer information in accordance with privacy laws like GDPR, CCPA, and CAN-SPAM. It requires obtaining proper consent, honoring opt-out requests, securing data, and providing transparency about how personal information is used.

### Which privacy laws apply to B2B companies?

B2B companies must comply with privacy laws based on where their customers are located, not where the business operates. Key regulations include GDPR (EU residents), CCPA/CPRA (California residents), CAN-SPAM (US email), and emerging state laws in Virginia, Colorado, Connecticut, and Utah. Even if you're US-based, processing EU customer data subjects you to GDPR.

### Is visitor identification GDPR compliant?

Yes—when implemented correctly. Visitor identification must include clear cookie consent banners, privacy policy disclosures explaining tracking practices, and mechanisms for users to opt out. Cursive's visitor identification platform is built GDPR-compliant, with consent management, opt-out handling, and data encryption as standard features.

### How do I handle GDPR data deletion requests?

Under GDPR Article 17, users can request deletion of their personal data. You must respond within 30 days, erasing their information from all systems unless legal obligations require retention. Cursive's data management tools let you search by email, view all associated records across integrated platforms, and execute permanent deletion with one click.

### What's the penalty for violating privacy regulations?

GDPR fines reach up to €20 million or 4% of global annual revenue, whichever is higher. CCPA allows up to $7,500 per intentional violation. Beyond fines, violations damage brand reputation, reduce customer trust, and can trigger civil lawsuits. The real cost is often lost business from customers who feel their privacy was violated.

### Do I need a Data Processing Agreement (DPA) with Cursive?

Yes. GDPR Article 28 requires DPAs with any third-party processor handling personal data on your behalf. Cursive provides enterprise-grade DPAs to all customers, documenting our security commitments, data handling procedures, and compliance obligations as a processor.

### How long should I retain marketing data?

Retention periods depend on legal requirements and business needs. Common guidelines: retain active customer data for the duration of the relationship plus 1-2 years, archive transactional records for 7 years (financial regulations), and delete inactive leads after 2 years. Document your retention schedule in your privacy policy.

### What happens if a vendor I integrate with has a data breach?

Under GDPR, you remain liable for how processors handle data. If a vendor experiences a breach, they must notify you within 72 hours, and you must assess whether user notification is required.
